Alan Turing

The father of theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence

Here's a time line of Turing's life:

  • 1912 - Born on June 23, 1912 in Maida Vale, London.
  • 1926 - Alan Turing first showed signs of intelligence and potential which was noticed at the Sherborne School.
  • 1931-1934 - Studied from 1931 to 1934 at King's College, in Cambridge. He was first in his class and was an honor student in the field of mathematics.
  • 1936 - Studied mathematics and cryptology while also building three of four stages of an electro-mechanical binary multiplier at Princeton University.
  • 1939 - Helped to solve the essential part of the German naval indicator system and also came up with the idea of sequential analysis.
  • 1940 - Was responsible for creating the Bombe which was a cryptanalytic machine used to decrypt the Enigma-enciphered messages being sent from the enemies.
  • 1942 -Traveled to the United States to work with the US naval cryptanalysts to create their version of the Bombe and an Enigma in Washington.
  • 1946 - He presented his paper in February of 1946 where he had presented the first detailed design of a stored program computer setup.
  • 1947 - Lived in Hampton, London where worked on the design of the ACE (Automatic Computing Engine).
  • 1948 - Was appointed a position in the mathematics department at the University of Manchester where he worked on software for one of the earliest stored-program computers. Created the LU decomposition method that was used and is still used today to solve matrix equations.
  • 1950 - The turing test was created in 1950. The turing test was created to compare the intelligence of a machine/computer to the intelligence of a human brain.
  • 1952 - Turing's book "The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis" was published in 1952 which was all about his interest in the biology of organisms.
  • 1954 - Turing committed suicide in his house where his housekeeper found him dead.

"A computer would deserve to be called intelligent if it could deceive a human into believing that it was human,"

-- Turing wrote in 1950 defining his now-famous Turing Test
